# code_3000
Collection of Assignments for CSE 3000: Contemporary Issues in Computer Science and Engineering

As a senior in college, I worked on a data visualization project where I took real governmental salary data from the state of Maryland and designed an interface for various metrics and statistics to be displayed. Something that caught my attention and will never leave me is that, in developing a simple predictive modeling decision tree, I observed that the predicted salaries would vary by 10-20% depending on whether the gender of the new data point indicated male or female. While this project was purely educational and had no real-world impact, the datasets I sourced from were both real and large. The dangerous precedent is that at the end of the day, data scientists or modelling engineers are the ones solely responsible for what features play a role in predictive modelling, such as in my case, but ALSO for what hidden relationships may exist between other features that disproportionately correlate with disadvantaged groups of people. 

As it stands today, no reasonable amount of law or oversight can prevent bias from AI models. Regardless of scale, whether on a single project for a small company or a large system deployed across millions of users, bias can still emerge through data and the features chosen to represent any problem. Moving forward, I believe ethical AI development requires scrutiny at every stage, from data collection to feature selection to evaluation.
